## ðŸ“ Part 1: The View From Above - Perspective as Power

### The Renaissance Revolution (1420)

**Filippo Brunelleschi** demonstrated linear perspectiveâ€”a mathematical system where all lines converge to a vanishing point. His experiment:

- Painted Florence Baptistery in perfect perspective
- Drilled a peephole at the vanishing point
- Viewed the panel's reflection in a mirror
- The painted image emerged seamlessly with reality

**Modern Parallels:** Viewfinder, camera, VR headset, camera obscura, photography, 3D scanners

### What Perspective Changed

**Human Position:** The viewer was placed at the center of vision. Space became measurable, ownable, navigable.

**Power Dynamics:** When land could be represented geometrically, it could be:

- Divided
- Taxed
- Sold
- Colonized

**Leonardo da Vinci's Imola Map (1502):** Unlike medieval maps centered on symbolic meaning, this used strict bird's-eye view. Commissioned by Cesare Borgia not to admire space, but to **control it**.

### The View From Above and Empathy

**What does viewing from above do to empathy?**

- Minimizes it
- Creates feeling of being surveyed/controlled
- Turns the world into abstraction

**Heidegger's Critique:** Modern humans no longer encounter things as beingsâ€”they see them as resources. Forests become timber inventory, rivers become hydropower. **Vision becomes a tool for capitalism.**

### Contemporary Artists Responding

**Andreas Gursky:** Adopts the elevated, impossible viewpoint of contemporary power itself

- Captures stock exchanges, factories, hotels, supermarkets from heights no human body could naturally reach
- Reveals capitalistic logic: sees masses, not individualsâ€”interchangeable data points
- **Warhol's insight:** The most effective way to disturb a system is to enter it and exaggerate it from within

**Mark Lombardi:** Hand-drawn diagrams of political and financial networks

- Exposes connections the powerful want hidden
- **Visibility becomes a political weapon**
- While Renaissance maps hid uncertainties with cartouches or sea monsters, Lombardi draws to expose

**Cubism's Challenge:** Fractured perspective, showing objects from multiple angles at once

- Later inspired **dazzle camouflage** in WWIâ€”a theory of vision became a tactic of invisibility

**David Hockney:** Photo collages stitch together multiple viewpoints and moments

- Rejected "tyranny of single point perspective"
- **"Perspective is not a natural law, it is a choice"**

**Key Takeaway:** Seeing is never neutral. Linear perspective reorganized the world into something measurable, ownable, governable. Artists respond by mimicking power's viewpoints to expose the logic.

## ðŸ“· Part 2: The Mechanical Eye - Photography and the Optical Unconscious

### The Horse's Secret (1879)

**Eadweard Muybridge's Bet:** During a horse gallop, do all four hooves ever leave the ground?

- Lined up cameras with trip wires
- Film revealed what no human eye had seen: the horse floating mid-air
- **From that moment on, machines know more about reality than we do**

**Reactions:**

- Painter Paul Delaroche: "From today painting is dead"
- Poet Charles Baudelaire: Photography is "refuge of failed painters"
- The public: **Enchanted**â€”photography democratized visibility

### The Optical Unconscious

**Dziga Vertov's "Kino-eye":** The camera's mechanical eye was more perfect than the human eye

**Walter Benjamin's "Optical Unconscious":** Just as Freud revealed unconscious desires driving behavior, the camera exposed unconscious structures within vision

**Artists Expanding Vision:**

- **Man Ray:** Placed objects directly on light-sensitive paper (rayographs)â€”toothbrush becomes spectral silhouette
- **Harold Edgerton:** Stroboscopic flash froze events faster than perception (bullet piercing glass, milk crown)
- **Michael Wesely:** Open shutter for _years_â€”buildings rise and vanish in a single frame
- **Karl Strover (1926):** Micrographsâ€”attached cameras to microscopes, captured diatoms and atoms
- **Ahmed Mater:** Overlaid hospital X-rays with Islamic illumination and prayerâ€”medical truth meets spiritual invocation
- **Trevor Paglen:** Photographs unseen surveillance architecture (secret satellite bases, undersea cables)â€”uses telescopic lenses to capture "stars" that are actually machines watching the planet

**Key Instruments:** Microscopes, telescopes, kaleidoscopes, X-rays, aerial photography, infrared, radio wavesâ€”all extensions of the photographic principle

## ðŸ‘» Part 3: Photography and Death - The Paradox of the Image

### Roland Barthes: Photography as Paradox

Every photograph says: **"Here it is" AND "This has been"**

- Every photo is a tiny time machine
- The essence of photography is **death**â€”a record of a moment that no longer exists
- Photos of the dead (or living) feel ghostly

**19th Century Literal:** Families posed deceased children for post-mortem photographsâ€”mechanical images began to replace lived memory

### The Overload Problem

**Erik Kessels - _24 Hours in Photos_ (2007):** Printed 350,000 images uploaded to Flickr in a single day, poured them into a gallery as a mountain

- **We're no longer rememberingâ€”we're drowning in memory**
- Photography's promise to preserve everything collapses into overload
- **Nietzsche's warning:** Too much memory is sickness

**Joachim Schmid:** Urges us to _stop taking pictures_

- Produces no new photographs
- Collects abandoned snapshots from flea markets, family albums, hard drives
- Reorganizes them into typologies (people holding cats, people in front of cars)
- Private memory becomes repeatable patternâ€”**life becomes a data set**

## ðŸ“º Part 4: Society of the Spectacle - When Images Become Events

### Guy Debord's Insight

"Everything that was directly lived has moved away into representation"

- History becomes what is photographed
- **The image no longer records the eventâ€”the image IS the event**

**1898 Example:** Photographers bribed their way into Otto von Bismarck's death chamber. Images circulated through newspapers, reaching mourning crowds who had never been present in life. The photograph didn't merely record historyâ€”**it manufactured a historical event**.

### Contemporary Fabrications

- In mass media, **legitimacy belongs to the frame, not the message**

**Thomas Demand:**

**Richard Prince - Marlboro Man:** Rephotographed cigarette ads

**Baudrillard's "Simulacra":** A world where copies no longer refer to originalsâ€”reality is completely replaced by representation

## ðŸŽ¬ Part 5: Montage - Controlling Sequence, Controlling Reality

### The Authority of Format

**Orson Welles - _War of the Worlds_ (1938):** Radio broadcast of fictional alien invasion in tone of live news

- Panic spread not because story was convincing, but because **format was**
- In mass media, credibility lies in the voice of transmission

### Eisenstein's Intellectual Montage

"Collision of images manufactures thought"

- One shot doesn't simply follow anotherâ€”it transforms it
- Meaning emerges **in the space between images**
- Example: _Battleship Potemkin_ Odessa Stepsâ€”no single shot can be interpreted alone

**Key Revelation:** When we watch film editing, it feels natural. We don't perceive we're being manipulated. We think **we** assemble the story correctly.

### The Narrative Imperative

**Christian Marclay - _The Clock_ (2010):** 24-hour video of thousands of film clips featuring clocks

- Every minute on screen = real time in gallery
- No plot, no storyline, no characters
- **Yet audiences anticipate what comes next and search for emotional continuity**

**Core Discovery:** "We cannot tolerate randomnessâ€”we turn it into meaning"

- Narrative is not only constructed by filmmakers

- No such thing as truly plotless filmâ€”our mind insists on creating one

**Andy Warhol - _Empire_ (1964):** 8-hour static shot of Empire State Building

- No plot, no action, no montage

- **Suspense emerges where there is no story**
- Exposes our dependency: we need events to justify attention

## ðŸ“º Part 6: Television - The Living Room Ideology Machine

### The Scale of Penetration

- 1953: 2/3 of American households owned TV
- 1960s: Almost 90%
- Average viewer: Hours every day in front of screen

**Susan Sontag (1977):** The rise of **"aesthetic consumerism"**â€”people need reality confirmed and experience intensified through images. To live increasingly meant to consume life photographically.

### Early Video Artists Fight Back

**Sony Portapak (1975):** First affordable handheld video cameraâ€”artists could record and display without film laboratories or broadcast studios

**Nam June Paik:** Treated television as both toy and weapon

- **Magnet TV:** Placed powerful magnet on working TVâ€”picture warped, collapsed into abstract currents. Evening news dissolved into pure signal. Revealed: what appeared authoritative was fragile electronic code

- **Electronic Superhighway:** Hundreds of monitors map the United Statesâ€”information becomes noise

**Wolf Vostell:** "Television is the sculpture of the 20th century"

- Embedded live monitors into canvases, metal, concrete
- Dragged TV out of living room into museum
- At home TV feels natural; in gallery it becomes strange and physical

### Who Is The Product?

**Answer:** The viewer

**Richard Serra - _Television Delivers People_ (1973):** White text scrolling on blue screen with elevator music

- "Television does not deliver programs to audiences. It delivers audiences to advertisers."
- Removing all visuals reveals the economic structure beneath the screen

### Artists Hijacking the Medium

**Douglas Davis - _The Last Nine Minutes_ (1977):** Addresses viewers directly through screen as if private conversation

- You feel seen but cannot respond
- **Paradox:** Simulates connection while keeping viewers isolated

**Dara Birnbaum - _Technology/Transformation: Wonder Woman_ (1978-79):** Cheerful commercials for facial tissues alternate rapidly with war/violence footage

- **TV's most dangerous trick:** Not censorship, but emotional leveling

**Chris Burden - _TV Hijack_ (1972):** Storms live broadcast studio

- Shows broadcast authority is fragile, can be interrupted physically

**Paper Tiger TV:** Built low-budget pirate programs dissecting mainstream news

- Used television to explain how television works

**Vladislav Mamyshev-Monroe:** Produced fake Soviet TV broadcasts perfectly mimicking official state media but with absurd, surreal, subversive content

- **Power lies less in what is said than how it's packaged**

**Media Burn:** Staged performances where car literally crashed into wall of TVs

- Not metaphorâ€”literal physical confrontation

**Marshall McLuhan:** "The medium is the message"â€”media are not simply channels, they are environments that shape perception

**Key Takeaway:** Images stopped documenting events and began producing them. Montage, broadcast, and mass media revealed: **whoever controls image sequence controls attention, thus controls reality**.

## ðŸ’» Part 7: Digital Logic - When Images Become Data

### Nancy Burson - The Calculated Face (1980s)

Pioneered digital morphing:

- Scanned faces with digitizer, translated portraits into numerical coordinates
- Computer distorted first face to match second, blending them
- **Not photographed, not paintedâ€”calculated**
- Co-created first patented facial morphing software (later adopted by FBI)

**Warhead 1:** Merged faces of Reagan, Brezhnev, Mitterrand, Thatcher
**2018 Time Magazine:** Merged Trump and Putin

**Result:** Human face becomes data set. Identity becomes something that can be computed, modified, generatedâ€”**no longer bound to a body**.

### Lev Manovich's Database Logic

Once photography showed reality can be frozen, cinema taught reality can be assembled through montage. **What does digital media do?**

Images are no longer fixed framesâ€”they are **modular units** (pixels, files, data sets) that can be stored, sorted, searched, recombined, generated.

**Database logic:** Meaning no longer arises from linear storytelling but from structure, correlation, accumulation, algorithmic recombination

### Manovich's Five Principles

1. **Numerical Representation:** Digital images are made of numbers
2. **Modularity:** Digital elements remain independent building blocks (clips, pixels, files can be rearranged without destroying whole)
3. **Automation:** Software can operate on images automatically
4. **Variability:** No digital image has singular formâ€”can be resized, recolored, recompressed, regenerated endlessly
5. **Transcoding:** Images exist simultaneously as visual culture AND computer data

### Artists Working with These Principles

**Dan Sandin - Sandin Image Processor (1971-74):** Analog video computer

- "Video equivalent of Moog synthesizer"
- Accepts video signals, modulates them through voltage/oscillation/feedback

- **Countdown:** Man receives daily input directly into head through TV signals
- **Departure:** Woman leaves because nothing good on television, but wanders lost without screen
- **Arrival:** Figures trapped in digital void of failed transmissions
- Bodies float like detached objectsâ€”proto-database where humans are searchable, non-interactive fragments

**Lev Manovich - _Soft Cinema_:** Builds database of video clips, images, text, soundtracks

**Key Takeaway:** Images are no longer visible things communicating content. They are modular elements that can be stored, sorted, recombined, generated. Meaning emerges from structure, correlation, permutation. **The image is no longer something we look atâ€”it's something that runs.**

## ðŸŽ¯ Conclusion: The Evolution of Vision

### Historical Progression

1. **Renaissance:** Vision became measurementâ€”to see is to possess. Human observer at center of world
2. **Mechanical Eye:** Machines revealed what human eye cannot see. Reality expanded but slipped out of our hands
3. **Memory & Archive:** Images began to remember. Past left our minds, entered archives/screens/networks
4. **Spectacle:** Images stopped recording events, began producing themâ€”shaping story, beliefs, identities
5. **Data:** Images became modular, generative, automatedâ€”no longer singular story, don't need single author

### Present State

**Seeing is no longer passive act. Seeing is:**

- An operation
- A computation
- A selection
- A targeting

**The Task of Artists Today:** Not simply to produce images, but to **interrupt the regimes of vision that produce us**
