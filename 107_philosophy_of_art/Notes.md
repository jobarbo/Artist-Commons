## Session 1: The Analytic Canon

The goal

1. Know the major theories - understand what they claim
2. Use them as tools - apply them to your own practice

The format:

- Talk + Reflection Questions + Q&a at the end.
- Post follow ups on Mighty Network.

The four core questions (to better understand my practice)

- **Quarter 1**: What am I making?
  - "I make stuff but is it art?"
  - -> "Here's what makes my work art and why."
  - Philosophical territory: ontology and definition
- **Quarter 2**: How does it mean?
  - "I hope people understand it..."
  - -> "Here's how my art creates meaning."
  - Philosophical territory: Semiotics and communication
- **Quarter 3**: Where do I fit?
  - "Everything has already beend done!"
  - -> "Here is my lineage & my contribution"
  - Philosophical territory: Originality and authorship
- **Quarter 4**: Why does it matter?
  - "Is my work saying anything important?"
  - -> "Here is why my work matters"
  - Philosophical territory: Value and function

Two philosophical traditions:

- Analytic philosophy
  - Emphasis on precise deifnitions, logical analysis, clear arguments
  - What IS {x}
    - Trying to find the boundaries of a category
- Continental philosophy
  - Emphasis on lived experience, historical context, how things show up for us
  - What does {x} DO?
    - What's at stake when we use a category?

These theories are canon and have a lot of problem (male and white centric)

- but they are still present in the industry of art.

---

Ontology is the philosphical study of what things ARE, or the study of BEING.

- What **kinds** of things exist? How should we **categorize** them?

**Ontology of art asks**:
"What IS art? What makes something art rather than something else? how do we define the category of art?"

### <u>Framework 1</u>

#### Art as Imitation / Mimesis

Art is : Things that imitate or represent the world (mimesis)

#### Plato: Art as imperfect Imitation.

Plato wasn't a big fan of art.

PLato at 3 tier of the essence of the world.

- Tier 1 : Forms (Perfect, eternal ideals )
  - Perfect circle, perfect concept of justice or beauty for example. Ideal realms
- Tier 2 : Physical world
  - Imperfect copies of the eternal ideals
- Tier 3 : Art
  - Copies of the imperfect copies.

So for Plato, art is an imitation of an imitation (shadow of a shadow)

"Artists traffic in appearances not truth"

#### Aristotle: Art as Teacher.

Art is imitation... but Imitation is not a deception
Imitation is how we learn.

Takeaway: **Art is a knowledge practice, not a lie**

The artist skills is measured by how well they can capture reality.

### <u>Framework 2</u>

#### Art as Aesthetic Form

Art is : Things that Create **aesthetic experience** through **formal properties**

#### <u>Immanuel Kant (1790)</u>

When you experience beauty , he calls it "Disinterested pleasure"

Not asking "can I use this" or "do I want to own this?"
Just contemplating. **Pure aesthetic attention**

**"Purposiveness without purpose"**

#### <u>Clive Bell (1914)</u>

He published a book called "ART"

Bell take Kant's idea about aesthetic experience and turn them into something he calls "Significant Form"

What all artworks share: Combinations of lines, colors, shapes that move us **aesthetically** (Art is aesthetic form)

#### <u>Clement Greenberg (1960's)</u>

Every art form should pursue what is unique to it.

**Medium Specificity**

Each art foprm should pursue its unique formal properties.

Ie. For painting: **Flatness**
Painting is pain on a flat surface, so modern painting should emphasize flatness, not create illusions of depth.

JB: Like when some people says that generative art should not take element from analog art.

### <u>Framework 3</u>

#### Art as Institutional Recognition

Art is : Things that get conferred **art-status** by the **art-world**

What makes it art is that the work get's treated as art.

The artworld is the institution that decides what's in and what's out.

#### <u>Arthur Danto (1964)</u>

Why are these Brillo boxes art when you can find them at the supermarket

**You can't tell what's art just by looking**

Something becomes art when it's presented in an art context, with art theories and art history behind it.

<u>Dickie's Institutional Theory</u>

Art is what gets conferred art-status by representatives of the artworld.

**Art is a status that gets conferred from outside the object, not a quality that lives inside it.**

Art is not intrinsic to the work but extrinsic.

<u>Bourdieu's Field Theory</u>

He developed the concept of cultural capital and field theory

The artworld is a field with its own logic, its own forms of value and its own gatekeepers.

Bourdieu's insight: Taste isn't natural or personal. it's socially constructed and reinforces power structures.

For artists it means that making good work isn't good enough. you need to accumulate cultural capital, be fluent in discource. Every field has it's own capital and gatekeepers and art is not different.

<u>Legitimation</u>

Basquiat was a graffiti artist, then he moved to gallery work and became one of the most succesful artist in history. once institutions started to embrace his work, they went back and appraised his street tag as well and some of them are now in museum.

The context didn't change the present, it rewrote the past.

#### <u>Andrea Fraser</u>

Museum Highlights (1989).

What makes the object in the museum art? (artowrk vs non art object like fountains)

**It is not a question of being against the institution. We ARE the institution**

<u>Understanding Cultural Capital</u>

- Credential Capital?
  - Do you have an MFA (where is it from)
- Institutional Affiliation?
  - IS your work shown in certain galleries
- Critical Validation?
  - Being written about?
- Linguistic Capital?
  - writing an artist statement using the right vocabulary
- Knowledge Capital?
  - Can you discuss you work in relationship to art history
- Social Capital?
  - Do you have connectionwith curators, collectors and galleries

None of this relate to the actual work of art.
